The Life of General George S. Patton

November 11, 1885

George Smith Patton Jr. was born in California.

1896-1902

Patton attended a private school where he enjoyed reading history, specifically military history. He learned of Hannibal, Scipio Africanus, Julius Ceasar, Joan of Arc, Napoleon Bonaparte, and a family friend, John Singleton Mosby.

1903-1904

Patton attended college at Virginia Military Institute.

1905

Senator Thomas Bard recommended Patton to West Point. He did well there in military drills but his academics were not the greatest. He had to repeat his first year because he failed his math class. He was very active in the school, playing football, joining the track team and the sword team.

June 11, 1909

Patton graduated from West Point as number 43 of 103 cadets. He became a second lieutenant in the US Army, Cavalry branch.

May 26, 1910

Age 24, Patton married Beatrice Banning Ayer. Over the course of the next 13 years they had 3 children.

1912

Patton competed in the Summer Olympics in Stockholm, Sweden in a pentathlon. He got fifth place.

After the Olympics, he attended a cavalry school in France where he learned more fencing techniques.

1913-1915

Patton designed the M1913 Cavalry Saber, also known as the "Patton Saber", using his knowledge from the French cavalry school.

He then attended the Mounted Service School at Fort Riley, Kansas. He was an instructor and a student there. He was the first to ever be titled "Master of the Sword".

March-April 1916

While stationed in Texas, Patton became an aide to John J. Pershing who commanded the Pancho Villa Expedition. Latter in life, Patton patterend much of his leadership after Pershing.

In April, Patton asked Pershing if he could command troops and was given Troop C of the 13th Cavalry.

May 14, 1916

Patton had his first combat experience. 9 days later, he is promoted to first lieutenant in the 10th Cavalry.

April 6, 1917

USA joined World War I.

Pershing became the commander of the American Expeditionary Force (AEF) on the Western Front.

May 15, 1917

Patton became a captain and left for Europe to be a part of Pershing's staff. However, he grew bored and his interest in tanks began to take off.

November 10, 1917

After being assigned to create the AEF Light Tank School, Patton traveled around Europe learning about tank operation and building, and he fought in several battles.

January 26, 1918

Patton was promoted to major. He was the only American soldier with tank experience when he was given 10 tanks and men to train.

April 3, 1918

Patton was promoted to lieutentant colonel and attended the Command and General Staff College.

August 1918

Patton got his first command of a tank unit, the 1st Provisional Tank Brigade (later renamed the 304th Tank Brigade). He lead this brigade through many battles for many months.

October 17, 1918

George Patton was promoted to colonel in title only.

November 1918-September 1940

Between World War I and World War II, Patton reverted to his pre-war rank of captain. He bounced around in the Army and slowly climbed the ranks back to colonel, he also attended more schooling in cavalry and command. He met Dwight D. Eisenhower in 1919. In 1937 Patton wrote a paper predicting a surprise attack on Hawaii by the Japanese. In 1938 George C. Marshall, who was the Army Chief of Staff, considered Patton for a promotion to general. However, to keep his command during this time of peace, he remained a colonel.

1940

World War II

Patton became the commander of the 2nd Armored Brigade and was in charge of training. On October 2nd, he was promoted to brigadier general. In November he became the acting division commander.

December 1940

Patton ran exercises with 1,000 tanks. He then did it again with 1,300 armored vehicles. He would fly a plane over these movements to observe them better and learn how to better deploy them.

April 4, 1941

Brigadier General Patton was promoted to major general and became the commanding general of the 2nd Armored Division.

Summer 1941

Patton was assigned by Eisenhower to help plan the invasion of Northern Africa, in Operation Torch. He commanded 33,000 men in 100 ships which landed in Morocco on November 8, 1942. They quickly took Casablanca, Morocco from Vichy French forces.

March 6, 1943

Patton was named Commanding General of the US II Corps and promoted to lieutenant general. Here began his campaign in Africa against German Generalfeldmarschall Erwin Rommel. On March 17 he won his first battle by taking Gafsa.

July 1943

Patton commanded troops in the invasion of Sicily, known as Operation Husky. He had helped plan this invasion as commander of the I Armored Corp stationed in Casablanca. This corps of 90,000 men was renamed the Seventh Army as they landed near Licata, Italy on the morning of D-Day, July 10, 1943. This campaign lasted just over a month.

January 26, 1944

Patton transfered to command the US Third Army. As they were a new army, his main task was to prepare the men for combat.

During this time, the Allied Nations used Patton's name as a diversion, spreading around Germany that he was the commander of the First United States Army Group (FUSAG) which was an army of decoys and props. The German's respected Patton and his command so much that they bought it and kept their troops in place to fight against his "invasion", leaving Normandy open and even after Normandy they stayed put waiting for his "attack".

August 1944

Patton's Third Army assisted the Allies as the extreme western force. They pushed hard and fast, trapping German troops with their push.

September-December 1944

The Lorraine campaign happened during this time, in which Patton took a German fort, Metz. This is considered one of his least successful campaigns.

December 1944

Patton moved some divisions of his Third Army to assist in a relief mission to Bastogne. Of this Patton wrote that it was "the most brilliant operation we have thus far performed".

Jaunary 29-March 22, 1944

Patton's army took seven German cities taking what remained of the German First and Seventh Armies. One city, Trier, he was told to pass and not take because it would take more troops than he had. However, the message arrived to late and he had taken it with half the troops they thought would be needed.

April 14, 1945

Patton was promoted to general. He marched towards Bavaria and Czechoslovakia for one last stand against the Germans. But he never made it before V-E Day on May 8, 1945.

June-November 1945

After being home in the States for less than a month, Patton was set as military governor of Bavaria and led his Third Army in the denazification there.

On September 28, 1945 he was relieved as a military govenor and a short while later (October 7), of his command of the Third Army. He then led the 15th Army in compiling a history of the war, but lost interest quickly, deciding instead to travel Europe.

On December 10th, Patton panned to leave Europe for good, and to resign from his command of the 15th Army.

December 1945

While on a hunting trip with his chief of staff, Patton was in a car accident with an army truck. He got a cut on his head and broke his neck, paralyzing him.

He passed away in his sleep on December 21, 1945 (age 60). He was buried in Luxembourg City by some of his men from the Third Army who died during the war.
